***Chapter 3: Advanced Topics and Ethical Considerations***

***Teaching Locomotion and Dynamic Movement***

Bipedal locomotion represents one of the most challenging aspects of humanoid robotics and offers rich pedagogical opportunities. Students begin by understanding static stability, where the robot's center of mass remains within its support polygon. They then progress to dynamic walking, learning about zero-moment point (ZMP) criteria, inverted pendulum models, and trajectory optimization. Implementing these concepts reveals the complexity of human walking—something we do effortlessly requires sophisticated control and sensing for robots. Advanced students can explore more recent approaches like model predictive control or learned locomotion through deep reinforcement learning, comparing classical and learning-based methods.


***Manipulation and Dexterous Interaction***

While locomotion moves the robot through space, manipulation enables interaction with objects. Teaching manipulation requires covering grasp planning, force control, and the coordination between perception and action. Students learn to implement visual servoing where the robot adjusts its movements based on camera feedback, experiencing firsthand how sensor latency and accuracy affect performance. Projects involving picking up objects of varying shapes, sizes, and materials demonstrate the challenge of generalizing manipulation skills. This naturally introduces discussions about the role of tactile sensing, compliance, and the vast gap between human dexterity and current robotic capabilities.


***Artificial Intelligence and Learning Systems***

Modern humanoid robots increasingly rely on machine learning rather than hand-coded behaviors. Educators must cover supervised learning for perception tasks like pose estimation and object recognition, reinforcement learning for developing locomotion and manipulation policies, and imitation learning where robots learn from human demonstrations. Students should implement simple neural networks, train them on collected data, and deploy them on physical systems. Critical discussions about data requirements, generalization challenges, computational constraints, and the difference between learning in simulation versus reality prepare students for the messy process of creating learning-based robotic systems.


***Human-Robot Interaction Principles***

Humanoid robots are designed to operate in human environments and interact with people, making human-robot interaction (HRI) a crucial course component. Students explore how robot appearance, movement, and behavior affect human perception and comfort. They learn about proxemics—appropriate social distances for robots—and design interaction modalities including speech, gesture recognition, and facial expressions. Projects might involve programming a robot to approach a person naturally, hand over an object safely, or respond appropriately to verbal commands. These exercises reveal the subtlety of human social conventions and the difficulty of encoding them in algorithmic form.


***Ethical Frameworks and Societal Impact***

Teaching physical AI requires addressing ethical considerations explicitly. Discussions should cover privacy concerns arising from robots with cameras and microphones in homes and public spaces, safety requirements for robots operating near humans, potential job displacement as robots take on tasks currently performed by people, and questions of accessibility—ensuring robotic technologies benefit diverse populations rather than exacerbating inequalities. Students can analyze case studies of robots deployed in elderly care, warehouses, or hospitals, debating appropriate applications and potential misuses. Encouraging students to consider not just what robots can do but what they should do develops responsible technologists who will shape the field's future.
